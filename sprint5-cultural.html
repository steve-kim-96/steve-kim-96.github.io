Little personal summary

Sprint 5, at least for me, was such a marathon. There was one day where I felt like I couldn't solve
the simplest coding problem... I felt like giving up or screaming, then I took a deep breath, told myself
even if it takes all night, I'm going to solve this. Sure enough the issue was a matter of extra quotation
marks LOL! I spent hours going over why my function wasn't working, but the problem was hidden in the
simplest form. I learnt a lot about patience, code, and most importantly, about myself that day.

I want to share A very simple quote that has shaped my mentality over the years:

Let's keep working hard, help each other, and build a bright future for each of us.

Why do I think EDA spends so much time on core when it’s a programming school:

As it explains from sprint 1, your mentality is key to programming. Programming, from my very little experience,
is such a big thing to tackle and it’s going to take a good long while before we can become proficient. Being able
to stay aware that it’s going to take months, even years to fully grasp everything there is to know about how to
solve programming problems is such a useful mindset to have, because it keeps you patient and willing to learn.

Does the core feel like a waste of time?

At first, I was skeptical and thought it was going to be a waste of time. But as time went on and as I spent more
and more time evaluating these personal questions and practicing mindfulness, I have come to think that it is
just as important as programming, if not more. These core skills can extend outside of programming,
bettering our lives, and in return improving our programming, so I think that more time could be spent on core than
currently is recommended.

Long term goal and/or career pathway?

All I know is that for now I want to enter the tech industry by getting a job, and eventually, somewhere down the line, I
want to be an entrepreneur. How that’s going to work, what I’m going to do? I have no idea.

My biggest strengths and limitations for bootcamp

Biggest strength is determination. If I’m stuck I won’t know how to give up until I’ve solved the problem.

Biggest limitation would be tunnel vision. I have a habit of falling into a specific line of thought. Luckily, the core skills have helped that habit.

Biggest non-technical challenge at bootcamp?

I need to be patient with others. One of the reasons I didn’t go into teaching is because I don’t like to slow down for others. If I go ahead, and
they lose pace then I don’t stop. I need to be able to empathise, stop, listen, and work together.

What non-technical skills would I like to develop at EDA?

Reflection is a big problem because I find it boring. I need to be able to stop, and think and experience what I did, how I did it, and what I could do
better next time.

Self-awareness is a work in progress but it’s helping me move mountains. I want to develop this skill even more.

Leadership skill is something that I’ve always wanted. When Obama gives his beautiful speeches, I think to myself “how can I do that?” What sort of skills
did he learn to become a leader?

What are my expectations of myself at bootcamp?

Not waste a minute of it. If I’m stuck, I need to use the problem-solving skills to get through it efficiently. If I’m ahead I need to take more time to reflect, and think
about how I can do better next time. What extra things could I learn?

I’ll seek help in a timely way by asking members of my cohort, and the tech facilitator and, most importantly, Googling

Three things Learnt about myself:

Through completing the core and programming exercises have taught me that I need to let go of the “I should know this” mentality. What I mean by this is, even the
simplest things can stump you. And different things stump different people. No matter how simple the exercise may be, be patient, let go of the mentality, and try to
learn as much as you can while you’re stuck.

Another thing I re-learnt about myself is how stubborn I am. I simply can’t let things go. Especially through the programming exercises, I’ve learnt that if I get stumped,
I can’t rest until I get the answer. I think it was both good and bad. Good in the way that I find the way to solve the problem through patience and determination. Bad
because the problem consumes my mind.

Also, I love to learn. I think I knew it deep down but I like learning. The more challenging, the better. I’m not afraid to ask for help, and I’m not embarrassed by the things
I don’t know. I go by the mentality “babies aren’t embarrassed by the fact that they can’t walk. And once they learn, it’s not too long before they start running”.

Role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming

Your values shape the way you think about things. I think that’s why it’s so important and so good that we talk about our values at EDA. Ultimately, how we tackle the
problems, and the mindset that we hold comes from our values.

Empathy determines the willingness to see outside of yourself. If someone is struggling with something, you don’t look down on them, but you can figure out why
it’s difficult, how that person is seeing a particular problem, and ultimately helping each other solve problems.

Self-awareness ties everything together. You need to be aware of your condition, your mentality so that you are working in an optimal headspace. If you’re stuck and you
have tunnel vision because of the length you’ve been stuck, you need to be able to realise it, take your mind off of the problem, and come back to it with a clear and
optimistic mind.

What surprised me the most about core learning

That it actually matters. I was one of those people that kind of thought “huh, is this actually going to help me?”

Man, was I wrong to doubt? Especially the self-awareness exercises, whenever I got stumped or on the verge of super annoyance, I was able to quickly escape from
the situation, recoup, and come back and eventually solve the problems.

The most challenging aspects of core learning, especially in the beginning, was trying to answer those questions about myself. I’d never really asked what kind of person
I am, what my values are, what my mentality is when I get stuck, and so on.

I’ll manage workload in the program by having a look at what’s expected each week and putting in a plan on how I will carry out those tasks, if more time is alotted to one task,
how I’ll manage the others. Ultimately, I won’t put a time limit. I’ll work no matter how long it takes to finish. I’ll work with others by talking through the problem, whether its theirs
or mine, and seeing what we can do to solve it. Same goes for facilitators and any other stakeholder I’ll be working with.

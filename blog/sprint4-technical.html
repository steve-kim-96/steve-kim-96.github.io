The time I got blocked on a simple problem:

During the time I was working on the kata, I came across a lot of problems. I've come to realize that some of them
were small problems. The one mentionable problem was during an exercise where I needed to create a collection of

elements called an object. All I had to do was use two little symbols "{}" which indicates the creation of an object
and then place my elements in them e.g. name, age, and so on. I thought about it for a while, even going through
previous exercises and I Googled "How to return an object". When I looked at the solution I couldn't believe that
I slipped on something so simple...

There are many listed ways of problem solving:
Pseudocode, trying something, Rubber Ducky Method, Reading error messages, Console.logging, Googling,
Asking peers, Asking coaches, Improving by reflecting, and so on.

I'm still working through the error message method but at the very least I can decipher where my problem
is, and for the most part, what's missing. The only problem I have with asking peers or coaches is sometimes
they aren't available and not everyone is in the position to help. I'm a bit of a weirdo so talking to myself or at
the wall/inanimate object has always been a problem solving method for everything. The most fun is just
trying things and seeing what they do and seeing how I can use them to solve my problem.

The time I solved a problem in an elegant way:

I solved a problem that involved taking a statement that was written backwards, and putting it the right
way. The smoothness of thought when solving this problem was just such a big moment for me. I'm not
saying I'm good at coding. Nowhere near, but my thought process was extremely clear solving this one.

First, I remembered that there was a method that allowed me to seperate a statement into a list of
individual words called the .split method. I then thought, how can I reverse the order and, at the same
time reverse the words themselves. Turns out there's a method called the .reverse method. And then
straight away, I remembered that in a previous exercise I practiced putting words and statements
together using the .join method.

Hopefully comes a time where I have enough experience that moments of clicking together like this
happens more often but that was an elegant and smooth way of solving that particular problem.
